## What was actually useful

**Demetrius: the "souls" idea**
Create a lightweight model of each audience member — drawing on their interview transcripts, their questions, their interests — and use it to query your knowledge graph from their perspective. Get the questions they'd ask. Get the follow-up questions. Use this to shape what you surface in a presentation. See [souls-technique.md](../souls-technique.md) for the full write-up.

**Robert: making invisible work visible**
His "dev update" skill — run it, get a whiteboard-style visual of what changed and what was done. A pebble out of his shoe. Ellie's observation: her partner built kitchen cabinets from scratch and there they were at the end of the day. She spent 10 hours in chat windows. The whiteboard idea is about making the invisible manifest.

**Robert: multiple-choice prompting**
Ask AI to offer multiple choice when it comes back to you, rather than open-ended responses. Even when none of the options are right, it reveals what you *don't* want — and that clarifies what you do. Reduces cognitive load on the orchestration loop.

**Ray: "love the ball"**
From *The Inner Game of Tennis* — when you hit the wall and all your frameworks fly out of your head, the one thought you can hold is "love the ball." Attention focuses, body moves toward it. In the messy middle: find the equivalent. What's the thing you can point your attention at that keeps you moving?

**Ray: shortening the swim**
The messy middle feels like swimming with no shore visible in either direction. Two responses: shorten the view (break it into shorter legs), and increase visibility (use tools to interrogate your own progress so you know how far you've come and how far there is to go).

**Demetrius: marathon metaphor**
Don't think about a marathon as a flat race. Think of it as a mountain run — you do it for the view at the peak, not the journey. When you hit the wall: look at where you were when you made the decision to start. Are you in a better position? Are you learning? Then keep going.
